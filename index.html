Clinical Protocol: Managing Neonatal Jaundice

Published: Oct 2024 | Category: Neonatology | Reviewed by: Clinical Team

1. Definition

Neonatal jaundice is the yellowish discoloration of the skin and sclera caused by the accumulation of unconjugated bilirubin in the blood (hyperbilirubinemia). It affects approximately 60% of term and 80% of preterm infants during the first week of life.

2. Causes & Risk Factors

Jaundice occurs when bilirubin production exceeds the liver's ability to clear it. Major causes include:

  • Physiological Jaundice: Normal breakdown of fetal RBCs and immature liver function (appears after 24 hours).
  • Pathological Jaundice: Appears within the first 24 hours, often due to:
    • ABO or Rh incompatibility.
    • G6PD deficiency.
    • Sepsis or internal hemorrhage (e.g., cephalohematoma).

3. Signs & Symptoms

The primary sign is the yellowing of the skin, which typically starts at the head and moves downward (cephalo-caudal progression). Other signs include:

  • Yellowing of the whites of the eyes (scleral icterus).
  • Poor feeding or lethargy.
  • High-pitched crying (a warning sign of bilirubin toxicity).
  • Dark urine or pale stools.

4. Assessment Modalities

Accurate measurement is vital to prevent Kernicterus (permanent brain damage).

  • Visual Inspection: Checking the blanching of the skin in natural light.
  • Transcutaneous Bilirubinometry (TcB): Non-invasive screening tool.
  • Total Serum Bilirubin (TSB): The gold standard blood test, required if TcB is high or jaundice appears early.

5. Management

Management Goal: Keep bilirubin levels below the threshold where neurological damage occurs.
Treatment Indication
Phototherapy Standard for most cases; uses blue-green light to convert bilirubin into water-soluble isomers.
Exchange Transfusion Emergency procedure for critically high bilirubin levels to prevent brain damage.
Hydration/Feeding Frequent breastfeeding (8-12 times a day) to encourage bowel movements and bilirubin excretion.

6. Prognosis

Most cases of neonatal jaundice are benign and resolve without treatment. However, the prognosis for Pathological Jaundice depends on the speed of intervention. With modern phototherapy, the risk of long-term complications is extremely low. If left untreated, severe hyperbilirubinemia can lead to Acute Bilirubin Encephalopathy (ABE) or permanent Kernicterus.
